Performances by the numbers

Two themes emerged from the data that can help us explore the state of The Canon: gender and nationalism. The canon is made up of DWGs (dead white guys) who lived and worked almost exclusively in Italy, Germany/Austria, France, and England. These include Gaetano Donizetti (1797-1848), George Frederic Handel (1685-1759), Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (1756-1791), Giacomo Puccini (1858-1924), Gioachino Rossini (1792-1868), Richard Strauss (1864-1949), Giuseppe Verdi (1813-1901), and, of course, Richard Wagner (1813-1883). Some of these names are ones that are probably familiar to you, even if you are not a musician. Much less well-known are the names of women composers, contemporary composers, composers from outside of Europe, and composers who were writing at the time of the DWGs but who for whatever reason didn't break through into the collective memory. Looking at how women composers as well as composers from non-canonical countries (countries other than the ones already mentioned) are represented in this data can offer us important insights on how closely artistic directors are sticking to the canon when they decide on their programs.

In this section, I aim give a quantitative introduction of the dataset before moving on to exploring the questions of gender and nationalism more fully in subsequent areas of this site. If you want to play around with the dataset on your own, I encourage you to head over to the Maps page, where you can filter by gender, nationality, composer, and work to see where in the world different trends emerged.

Distinct Works

Over the course of six seasons, the opera companies represented in this data performed 2,856 distinct works. However, this number does not account for multiple works with the same title written by different composers; for example, La Boheme is the title of a famous Puccini opera, but it is also the title of an opera based on Puccini's and written by Turkish composer Sinem Altan. Taking into account doubled works, then, the true number may be closer to 3,000.

Due to the cost (of both time and money) of putting on an opera, many companies will program a small number of works each season, but perform each one multiple times. The multiple performances of any given work by any given company in any given season is called a "run". In general, the longer the run, the more popular the work is, since it indicates that the company felt sure that they would sell enough tickets for each performance. New works tend to get shorter runs for this reason - it can be a risk to program a work that no one has ever heard of. This chart shows the top 100 works, based on the number of times they were performed:

Unsurprisingly, most of these works are in Italian, and many of them are as well-known as their composers. Thanks to programs like Looney Toons and Tom and Jerry, children in the United States have grown up with this music for decades. (See: Barber of Seville, Die Fledermaus, Ride of the Valkyries.) Of the works in this list, 43% were written by just 5 composers: Verdi (13), Wagner (9), Mozart (8), Puccini (8), and Donizetti (5). In this visualization, you can see each composer who is represented in the top 100 set:

Georges Bizet (1838-1875) stands out in this visualization because of one opera: Carmen, which was performed 4,094 times. Other standouts are Johann Strauss II (1825-1899), because of the popularity of Die Fledermaus, and Franz Lehár (1870-1948), because of his best-known work, Die Lustige Witwe (The Merry Widow). Among the other famous operas on the list are Hansel und Gretel (Humperdinck), Pagliacci (Leoncavallo), Roméo et Juliette (Gounod), A Midsummer's Night Dream (Britten), Orfeo ed Euridice (Gluck), and Samson et Dalila (Saint-Saëns). None of these operas were composed by a woman and none of these composers are still alive.

Eras of Music History

We can start to get a good idea of how the canon is developed by looking at composers from different eras in music history. Only a few composers withstand the passage of time - those that do tend to be the ones that make it into the canon, like the composers listed above. 

This chart shows the spread of composers based on their birth year. We can use this information to get a rough idea of which musical eras are the most popular for programming:

Renaissance: c. 1400 - c. 1600
Baroque: c. 1600 - c. 1750
Classical: c. 1750 - c. 1830
Early Romantic: c. 1830 - c. 1860
Late Romantic: c. 1860 - c. 1920
Post War Years: c. 1920 - present

The following visualization breaks down the timeline above into the distinct eras so you can see what's going on more easily.

The highest spike is in the Classical Period for Verdi. There were nearly 26,000 performances of Verdi operas over the six years captured in this dataset. Despite the cluster of composers in the modern era, it is clear that in terms of frequency, composers from the past are more popular with the people who make the decisions about what to program. It is important to note, too, that the cluster of modern composers correlates to fewer performances - whereas a few canonical composers are performed thousands of times, lots of modern composers are played only a few times.  Moreover, if you pay any attention to opera companies, you'll notice that "new" works are almost always newly written; it is very rare that a work from a long-dead composer will be revived and reintroduced, which is why there are bigger spikes spaced farther apart in the past. Combined with what we already know about how often new works get performed, this is how the canon stays intact despite the introduction of new composers into the mix.

Works Written By Women


In the six seasons included in this data set, only 154 of the works performed were written by women, compared to the 2,711 that were written by men. Most of those works were written by women writing in the Late Romantic period or later, which we can see easily when we isolate female composers in the spread:

Very few women made it out of the past. From the Renaissance Era, only Francesca Caccini (1587-c.1641) was programmed: four companies performed just one of her works for a total of eight performances. After her, no other woman appears until Pauline Viardot in 1821 (twelve performances of one work, by six companies) and, after another gap, Ethel Smyth in 1858 (eleven performances of two works, by two companies). By comparison, 31 of Verdi's works enjoyed 19,871 performances by 697 different companies. 

It is not the case that women weren't composing during those periods. Their work just simply has not been chosen for programming. Famously, the New York Metropolitan Opera performed Ethel Smyth's opera Der Wald in 1903 and then took a 113 year hiatus before performing another work written by a woman: in 2016, they finally broke their streak and performed L'amour de Loin by Finnish composer Kaija Saariaho (b. 1952). [1] Saariaho, in fact, was the female composer in this dataset whose works were played most frequently - over six seasons, thirteen companies performed seven of her compositions a total of sixty-four times, a number that still pales in comparison to Verdi.
