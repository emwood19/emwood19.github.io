Performances by the numbers

Main Themes

Two themes emerged from the data that can help us explore the state of The Canon: gender and nationalism. The Canon is made up of DWGs (dead white guys) who lived and worked almost exclusively in Italy, German, and England. These include Ludwig van Beethoven (1770-1827), George Frederic Handel (1685-1759), Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (1756-1791), Giacomo Puccini (1858-1924), Gioachino Rossini (1792-1868), Richard Strauss (1864-1949), Giuseppe Verdi (1813-1901), and, of course, Richard Wagner (1813-1883). These names are ones that even non-musicians have heard of. Much less common are the names of women composers, contemporary composers, composers from outside of Europe, and composers who were writing at the time of the DWGs but who for whatever reason didn't break through into the collective memory. Looking at how women composers as well as composers from other countries are represented in this data can offer us important insights on how closely artistic directors are sticking to The Canon when they decide on their programs.

In this section, I aim give a general overview of the dataset and to break down each of these questions by number before moving on to exploring them more fully in subsequent areas of this site.

Distinct Works

Over the course of six seasons, the opera companies represented in this data performed 2,856 distinct works. However, this number does not account for multiple works with the same title written by different composers; for example, La Boheme is the title of a famous Puccini opera, but it is also the title of an opera based on Puccini's and written by Turkish composer Sinem Altan. 

This chart shows the top 100 works by popularity, based on the number of companies that performed the work (i.e. the number of times the work appeared as a line in the dataset). Of these, 51% were written by just seven composers: Verdi (13), Wagner (9), Mozart (8), Puccini (8), Donizetti (5), Rossini (4), and Richard Strauss (4). Among the famous operas on the list not written by one of those seven are Carmen (Bizet), Hansel und Gretel (Humperdinck), Pagliacci (Leoncavallo), Roméo et Juliette (Gounod), A Midsummer's Night Dream (Britten), Orfeo ed Euridice (Gluck), and Samson et Dalila (Saint-Saëns). None of these operas were composed by a woman, and none of these composers are still alive. 

Eras of Music History

We can start to get a good idea of how The Canon is developed by looking at composers from different eras in music history. Only a few composers withstand the passage of time - those that do tend to be the ones that make it into The Canon, like the composers listed above. 

This chart shows the spread of composers based on their birth year. We can use this information to tell us about which musical eras are the most popular for programming:

Renaissance: c. 1400 - c. 1600
Baroque: c. 1600 - c. 1750
Classical: c. 1750 - c. 1830
Early Romantic: c. 1830 - c. 1860
Late Romantic: c. 1860 - c. 1920
Post War Years: c. 1920 - present

The highest spike is in the Classical Period for Verdi. There were nearly 26,000 performances of Verdi operas over the six years captured in this dataset. Despite the cluster of composers in the modern era, it is clear that in terms of frequency, composers from the past are more popular with the people who make the decisions about what to program. It is important to note, too, that the cluster of modern composers correlates to fewer performances - whereas a few Canonical composers are performed thousands of times, lots of modern composers are played only a few times.

Works Written By Women


In the six seasons included in this data set, only 154 works written by women were performed, compared to 2,711 written by men. Most of those works were written by women writing in the Late Romantic period or later, which we can see easily when we isolate female composers in the spread:

Very few women made it out of the past. From the Renaissance Era, only Francesca Caccini (1587-c.1641) was programmed: four companies performed just one of her works for a total of eight performances. After her, no other woman appears until Pauline Viardot in 1821 (twelve performances of one work, by six companies) and, after another gap, Ethel Smyth in 1858 (eleven performances of two works, by two companies). By contrast, 31 of Verdi's works enjoyed 19,871 performances by 697 different companies. 

It is not the case that women weren't composing during those periods. Their work just simply has not been chosen. Famously, the New York Metropolitan Opera performed Ethel Smyth's opera Der Wald in 1903 and then took a 113 year hiatus before performing another work written by a woman: in 2016, they performed L'amour de Loin by Finnish composer Kaija Saariaho (b. 1952). Saariaho, in fact, was the female composer in this dataset whose works were played most frequently - over the six seasons, thirteen companies performed seven of her compositions a total of sixty-four times, a number that pales in comparison to Verdi.
