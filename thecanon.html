An alternative canon

So, what have we learned from all of this? Not a whole lot. The two markers of the Western opera canon - gender (male) and race/nationality (white European) - are still deeply engrained in the choices of artistic directors and programmers. While there has been some improvement in the representation of women and people from non-canonical countries among opera composers, there is still a long way to go. And unfortunately, until patrons - those who fund these performances - start demanding change, we are likely to continue to see thousands of yearly performances of Verdi. 

On that note, rather than spending the rest of this page rehashing this project, I'd like to offer an alternative. You can think of this as a ready-made demands list to present to your local opera company when they ask what kinds of work you want to see next season. (Or, you know, even if they don't ask.)

An Alternative Canon

I created this list by first removing all composers of Italian, Austrian, German, Russian, French, and British heritage in an effort to highlight composers from countries not well-represented within the canon. I then removed all composers who were designated as male in an effort to highlight the works of women, who have struggled to gain a foothold in a male-dominated world. The result is a list of just under 100 works written by 80 women. I have included recordings of select works when available.

To be clear, this is not to say that women (and men) from canonical countries and men from non-canonical countries have not produced important work, nor is it to say that we should completely forget about the masterpieces created by the DWGs. Rather, the goal is simply to make more room at the table. I hope that by highlighting these women's work, our collective idea of what "opera" is can expand until there is not so much reliance on the likes of Donizetti, Handel, Mozart, Puccini, Rossini, Strauss, Verdi, and Wagner.
