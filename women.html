Where are the women?

In the six seasons represented in this dataset, women composed just 154 works out of a total of 2,865. That's 5.4%, a truly abysmal showing. Here's what that number looks like on a pie chart:

It's even worse when we look at the number of performances. Over the six seasons in this dataset, works by men were performed 140,928 times; women, just 1,093. The slice of pie to represent that percentage - 0.78% - is all but invisible.

This means that not only were fewer works by women programmed, those that were programmed were given shorter runs than works composed by men. So what gives? To unpack this overwhelming underrepresentation, we have to look at the history of women with regard to music in general and opera in particular.

Women and the Opera

Women have had a fraught history with the public sphere of music. The traditional role for women was in the private or domestic sphere, so even though many (upper-class) women were encouraged to cultivate musical talent, the assumed context was for at-home entertainment - playing the piano after dinner or for small gatherings. Composition, publishing, and performing, on the other hand, were part of the public sphere, which was traditionally reserved for men. That is not to say that no women composed music or published - for example, Clara Schumann (1819-1896) was a renowned composer and pianist, but after her marriage to fellow composer Robert Schumann (1810-1856), she backed away from public life. (There has been speculation that some of Robert Schumann's later works were actually written by Clara Schumann and published under his name.) 

In opera, this public/private division meant women often were not even cast for female roles. Beginning in the 16th century, castrati (men who were castrated as young boys, before puberty, so as to retain the soprano vocal register) were used for most female roles. [1] To be clear about what this meant: men were so averse to the idea of women occupying public space that they decided that the mutilation of young children was preferable to having a woman onstage. It is no wonder, then, that so few women composers enjoyed any sort of prominence during the canonical years of opera. Even after castrati fell out of favor in the 18th century and the star of the prima donna rose, opera composition remained a man's world. [2] 

This historical context supplies one argument often given for the lack of female composers from before the modern era being programmed in opera houses around the globe, as seen by the timeline introduced in the last section:

This explanation, though, relies on the idea that there were no female opera composers between Francesca Caccini in the Renaissance Era and Pauline Viardot in the Early Romantic Era, which is simply not true. In the last several decades, scholars have worked to uncover the compositions of women who defied the gender norms of their time to write operas. Jacqueline Letzter and Robert Adelson note that, "In the first fifty years of opera in France (1670-1720), only approximately seven works by three women are known... In the next fifty-year period (1720-70), the figures remain relatively stable with nine operas by four women. But in the fifty years between 1770 and 1820, at least forty-four operas by nineteen women are known to have been composed or performed." [3] That's twenty-six composers and sixty operas, just in France. Letzter and Adelson's paper is 21 years old, so the "we didn't know about them" excuse cannot be used for the six seasons from 2012-2018 that comprise this dataset.

Another Explanation

A (perhaps) unsurprising trend emerged from the data that could offer another explanation for the lack of women: of the 75 countries represented, more than half of them (43 - that's 57%) programmed no women composers whatsoever. 

While that's a big deal, those 43 countries are only responsible for just under 8% of the number of performances over the six seasons: 

This means that, had the countries who programmed works by both men and women programmed more gender diversity, we might not be seeing the discrepancy that currently exists. It is notable that many of those countries are Western countries who have undergone women's rights movements of some variety. For example, the United States, which underwent a famous women's liberation movement in the 1960s and 1970s, programmed 10,044 works by men, but only 163 by women. Similar discrepancies exist in France (6,944 to 77), Germany (42,058 to 251), and the United Kingdom (5,969 to 69), all of whom have similar histories to the US when it comes to women's rights. This visualization shows every country broken down by gender, so that some countries have two bubbles. You can search for a country on the right-hand side of the chart, and one or two bubbles will highlight for each one so you can see the discrepancy, even among so-called "liberated" countries.

Conversely, the countries who performed works only by men tended to be in the global South, where patriarchal power structures tend to be firmly intact. Those countries are also ones in which opera is not historically relevant, and the confluence of those two factors means that it is not necessarily surprising that no works by women have been programmed in those countries.

On the other hand, New Zealand is on the list of countries who performed no works by women. As a country who has had multiple women in its highest office (a status the United States cannot claim) and that was colonized by a Western country just as Australia, Canada, and the United States were (a process that presumably included the introduction of Western art), it is not unreasonable to expect that they might have performed more works by women. However, opera was simply not very popular in New Zealand until around 1954, when the first professional opera company was formed. [4] This can serve as a warning that the reasons behind why a country performed no works by women may not be obvious or stereotypical. To make lasting change in the representation of female composers in opera, we need to be focusing on those countries where opera is performed most frequently, and where lip service is most often paid without action to marginalized communities.
